# Typed Workspaces as Container-Level Architecture

## Research Question

Should garden patches carry explicit types that describe the kind of interaction they support? Victoria Gracia's Uni-Versum provides a taxonomy (insula, taberna, horreum, living books); the estate already runs typed workspaces in practice but hasn't named them. The question is whether formalizing container-level typing adds architectural value, and if so, what the estate's own type vocabulary looks like.

## Current State

Victoria's taxonomy names four workspace types:

- **Insula** (relational space) — exists because of a relationship with a person, project, or idea. Has its own agents, sources, and rhythm.
- **Taberna** (shared workshop) — collaborators actively building together. Ideas in motion; nodes created, questioned, revised.
- **Horreum** (harvested archive) — crystallized reasoning. Stable, referenceable, done for now.
- **Living books** — a reading becomes a workspace that grows as understanding grows. Produces not just notes but an expert: an agent internalized from the source material.

The estate already has garden patches that fit these types without naming them:
- The **persona garden patch** is a taberna (active collaborative workspace) that will become part-horreum once Thursday's work is published.
- **Field Notes** (spiralrhapsody.com/fieldnotes) has a different interface — clickable sounds — but is a garden patch underneath.
- The **Group Works Pattern Language** update will present as a purpose-built website but is also a garden patch. Planned interactivity (comments, co-editing) pushes it toward taberna.
- Research notes and citation dossiers in the garden are horreum-like: accumulative, stable, consulted rather than evolved.

Victoria handles maturity at the container level (what kind of space is this?). The estate handles maturity at the node level (seed, growing, evergreen, published). Both axes are needed and neither alone is sufficient.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the estate's own type vocabulary?** Victoria's Latin terms serve her system. The estate may need its own names, or may adopt hers, or may find a different classification axis entirely.

2. **How does container-level status interact with node-level status?** A patch full of seed-stage nodes is a workshop. A patch full of evergreen nodes is an archive. Does the container status derive from its contents, or is it independent? The BC Research repo's status system suggests container status is hard to maintain — it drifts from content reality.

3. **Does "living books" generalize?** Christopher's instinct: the pattern — deep engagement with source material producing both workspace and specialized agent — extends beyond books to podcasts, codebases, graphic novels, documentaries, research traditions, long-running conversations. Victoria demonstrated this with Sonja (an agent born from reading Toki Pona). What is the general pattern?

4. **Does the "living X" pattern produce agents?** Victoria's living books produce agents (Sonja). Her Co-Co agent processes arriving messages and synthesizes thematically. If container types can produce specialized agents, the container is not just a passive space but an active participant in the knowledge system.

5. **Where does ecosystem-vs-tended-garden fit?** Victoria distinguishes knowledge the gardener brings (tended) from knowledge that arrives (ecosystem). The estate handles arrivals through clippings (accumulated passively) promoted to citations (actively elevated). This is a garden-with-membrane pattern, not an open ecosystem. Is Victoria's ecosystem model complementary or competing?
